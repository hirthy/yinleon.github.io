Life Begins Adrift

3 billion years ago, the primodial Ocean gave rise to the Earth's first species, cyanobacteria. The rise of these drifting plants- also known as Phytoplankton, effectively introduced the oxygen and food sources necessary to support complex life. Phytoplankton continue to produce half the oxygen we breathe and helps the Ocean sequester one third of anthropogenic carbon dioxide we create. With that in mind, the Ocean is a significant carbon reservoir; containing 50 times more carbon than the atmosphere, and 10 times more carbon than all the world’s fossil fuels!

Biologic Pump

Although Phytoplankton in the Ocean’s surface only hold 3 pg of carbon, they contribute 11 pg of carbon into the deep Ocean in the form of sinking dead material called detritus. This inequality suggests a rapid cycle of production and loss, observable in seasonal Phytoplankton blooms. This biological phenomenon, called the Biological pump is driven by physical forces and regulates Ocean's organic carbon cycle. In areas of coastal upwelling, anthropogenic intervention can push the equilibrium of growth and loss exasperating contemporary environmental issues such as hypoxia and Ocean acidification.

Ocean Acidification

The Atmosphere and Ocean actively compete for equal treatment. This childish dispute is especially problematic in the wake of rising atmospheric CO2 levels. As the Ocean surface races to dissolve CO2, reactions occur in the seawater, creating corrosive conditions that reduce the supply of building blocks necessary for the calcification of functional structures. Due the susceptibility of shellfish and nurseries ecosystems (such as coral) Ocean acidification threatens the United States’ 35 billion dollar fishing and aquaculture industry.

Arctic Circulation

The ins-and-outs of this tiny land-enclosed sea are notoriously complex, with warm surface waters coming in from the Pacific and Atlantic, and cold subsurface waters exiting only into the Atlantic. The formation of cold deep waters are essential for moderating the Global heat flux. As the Arctic warms, the increased influx of fresh meltwater creates profound feedbacks on the rate at which the sea ice receeds and the strength of vertical mixing gradients. The unique origins and pathways of circulation are dependent on topography, geography and hydrology, but can be explored using reigonal linear relationships between oxygen-18 and salinity.

Next Steps

As Arctic sea ice cover hit it's fourth lowest in history, lucrative reserves of natural resources and shipping routes are increasingly accessible. However, the retreating ice has largely unknown biogeochemical, ecological and economic reprecussions. The Arctic is warming twice as fast as the planet as a whole. Sea ice is predicted to disappear by the middle of the century. To discern what's to come, nuances in data analysis, remote sensing and numerical modeling must be applied to understand the interface between the sea, ice and air. Like the Biological pump, we can build off physics to understand complex chemical and biological responses.
