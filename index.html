Research

There are many ways to investigate chemical exchanges in the Envrionment. Life Begins Adrift

3 billion-some years ago, the primodial Ocean gave rise to the Earth's first species, cyanobacteria. The rise of these drifting plants-also known as Phytoplankton, effectively introduced the oxygen and food sources necessary to support complex life. Phytoplankton continue to produce half the oxygen we breathe and helps the Ocean sequester one third of anthropogenic carbon dioxide we create. With that in mind, the Ocean is a significant carbon reservoir; containing 50 times more carbon than the atmosphere, and 10 times more carbon than all the world’s fossil fuels!

Biologic Pump

Although Phytoplankton in the Ocean’s surface only hold 3 pg of carbon, they contribute 11 pg of carbon into the deep Ocean in the form of sinking dead material called detritus. This inequality suggests a rapid cycle of production and loss, observable in seasonal Phytoplankton blooms. This biological phenomenon, called the Biological pump is driven by physical forces and regulates Ocean's organic carbon cycle. In areas of coastal upwelling, anthropogenic intervention can push the equilibrium of growth and loss exasperating contemporary environmental issues such as hypoxia and Ocean acidification.

Ocean Acidification

The Atmosphere and Ocean actively compete for equal treatment. This childish dispute is especially problematic in the wake of rising atmospheric CO2 levels. As the Ocean surface races to dissolve CO2, reactions occur in the seawater, creating corrosive conditions that reduce the supply of building blocks necessary for the calcification of structural and vital physiological structures. Due the susceptibility of shellfish and nurseries (such as coral) Ocean acidification threatens the United States’ 35 billion dollar fishing and aquaculture industry.

Arctic Circulation

The circulation of this small enclosed sea is notoriously complex, with fresh Pacific water entering through the Bering strait and salty Atlantic water entering through the Fram Strait. These warm equatorial waters cool and sink as they come in contact with sea ice. These fresh cool waters travel south along the Greenland coast and Canadian archipelago into the North Atlantic in a process known as the Ocean Conveyour Belt, playing a vital role in Global Circulation. The unique origins and pathways of circulation is dependent on topography, geography and hydrology and can be tracked using relationships between oxygen-18 and salinity.

Next Steps

The the Arctic is especially sensintive to climate change. As the Arctic sea ice hit it's fourth lowest in history, lucrative reserves of natural resources and shipping routes have become increasingly accessible. Unfortunaetly the retreating terrestrial and sea ice has relatively unknown biogeochemical, ecological and economic reprecussions. The Arctic is warming twice as fast as the planet as a whole. Sea ice is predicted to be completely gone by the middle of the century. To discern what's to come nuances in remote sensing and numerical modeling must be applied to quantify the baseline and future in the inferface of sea, ice and air. I hope to find what is shared and unique among the hemispheres to get a better picture of Polar Oceanography in general.
