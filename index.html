Arctic Circulation

The circulation of this small enclosed sea is notoriously complex with fresh Pacific water entering through the Bering strait (between Alaska and Russia) and salty Atlantic water entering through the Fram Strait (between Greenland and Europe). These warm equatorial waters cool down and sink as they come in contact with Sea ice. Some of these waters manage to escape along the Greenland coast and Canadian archipelago, while others are entrained in the Beaufort Gyre along the Canadian coast. The origins and pathways of circulation is dependent on the topography, geography, and hydrology involved in the formation of water masses.

Life Begins

Almost 3 billion years ago, the Ocean gave rise to the the first species on Earth, cyanobacteria. Phytoplankton like cyanobacteria, effectively introduced oxygen into Earth’s early atmosphere and the carbohydrates necessary to support complex life. Phytoplankton continue to produce half the oxygen we breathe and helps the Ocean sequester one third of anthropogenic carbon dioxide. With that in mind, the Ocean is a significant carbon reservoir; containing 50 times more carbon than the atmosphere, and 10 times more carbon than all the world’s fossil fuels!

Ocean Acidification

One Consequence of the Ocean's knack of carbon sequestration is Ocean acidification. The Atmosphere and Ocean act as jealous siblings competing for equal treatment. This childish dispute is especially problematic in the wake of rising atmospheric CO2 levels. As the Ocean races to match the Atmosphere, dissolved CO2 reacts with seawater creating carbonic acid and consuming the dissolved building blocks necessary for the structural calcification and physiological functionality of economically valuable species.
