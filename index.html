Introduction

I am interested in building tools to model the physical, chemical and biological exchanges brokered between the sea, ice and atmosphere. This global stock market is often referred to as Biogeochemistry and it has as much to do with you and I, as the sun in the sky.

Research

Biogeochemistry is a broad field involving anything from physilogical responses on a molecular level to physical drivers on a planetary level. I use scientific computing and data analytics to connect the gaps between these disciplines. Projects I've participated in the past include: building scalable frameworks to leverage stable isotopes to trace Ocean currents in the Arctic, utilizing simple 1D numerical models to simulate Phytoplankton blooms and designing empirical experiments to prepare for the ecological and economic costs of an increasingly acidic Ocean.

Life Begins Adrift

3 billion years ago, the primodial Ocean gave rise to the Earth's first species, cyanobacteria. The rise of these drifting plants- also known as Phytoplankton, effectively introduced the oxygen and food source necessary to support complex life. Phytoplankton continue to produce half the oxygen we breathe and helps the Ocean sequester one third of anthropogenic carbon dioxide we create. Becuase the Ocean contains 50x more carbon than the atmosphere, and 10x more carbon than all the world’s fossil fuels, it is a signifigant carbon resevoir!

Biological Pump

Although Phytoplankton on the Ocean surface only hold 3 pg of carbon, they export 11 pg of carbon into the deep sea as they die and sink in the weeks following Phytoplankton blooms. This rapid cycle of production and loss is observable from space as elegant green swirls in the late Winter and early Fall. This biologic mosaic is shaped by physical forces (sunlight and calm waters) and regulates the Ocean's carbon cycle. Like the mysterious origins of cancer in the human body, we are unsure what throws the system out of whack. Evidence from the Chesepeake Bay and Gulf of Mexico suggest agro-runoff tips the scale and causes hypoxia and Ocean acidification.

Ocean Acidification

The Atmosphere and Ocean actively compete for equal treatment. This childish dispute is especially problematic in the wake of rising atmospheric CO2 levels. As the Ocean surface races to dissolve CO2, reactions occur in the seawater, creating corrosive conditions that reduce the supply of building blocks necessary for the calcification of essential structures. Due the susceptibility of shellfish and coral ecosystems, Ocean acidification threatens the United States’ 35 billion dollar fishing and aquaculture industry.

Arctic Circulation

The ins-and-outs of this tiny land-enclosed sea are notoriously complex. Warm surface waters enter from the Pacific and Atlantic, while cold subsurface waters exit only into the Atlantic. The formation of cold deep waters are essential for moderating the Global heat flux. As the Arctic warms, the increased influx of fresh meltwater creates profound positive feedbacks on excellerating the rate of sea ice loss and altering global weather patterns. Tracing the origins and pathways of these feebcaks involves understanding Arctic circulation. which can be done using reigonal linear relationships between oxygen-18 and salinity .

Next Steps

As Arctic sea ice cover hit it's fourth lowest in history, lucrative reserves of natural resources and shipping routes are increasingly accessible. However, the retreating ice has largely unknown biogeochemical, ecological and economic reprecussions. The Arctic is warming twice as fast as the planet as a whole. Sea ice is predicted to disappear by the middle of the century. To discern what's to come, nuances in data analysis, remote sensing and numerical modeling must be applied to understand the interface between the sea, ice and air.
