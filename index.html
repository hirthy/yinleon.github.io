Introduction

I am interested in building tools to model the physical, chemical and biological exchanges brokered between the sea, ice and atmosphere. This global stock market is often referred to as Biogeochemistry and it has as much to do with you and I, as the sun in the sky.

Research

Biogeochemistry is a broad field involving anything from physilogical responses on a molecular level to physical drivers on a planetary level. I use scientific computing and data analysis to connect the gaps between these disciplines. Projects I've participated in the past include: building scalable frameworks to leverage stable isotopes to trace Ocean currents in the Arctic, utilizing simple 1D numerical models to simulate Phytoplankton blooms and designing empirical experiments to prepare for the ecological and economic costs of an increasingly acidic Ocean.

Life Begins Adrift

3 billion years ago, the primodial Ocean gave rise to the Earth's first species, cyanobacteria. The rise of these drifting plants- also known as Phytoplankton, effectively introduced the oxygen and building blocks necessary to support complex life. Phytoplankton continue to produce half the oxygen we breathe and helps the Ocean sequester one third of anthropogenic carbon dioxide. Becuase the Ocean contains 50x more carbon than the atmosphere and 10x more carbon than all the world’s fossil fuels, it is a signifigant carbon resevoir!

Biological Pump

Although Phytoplankton on the Ocean surface only hold 3 pg of carbon, they export 11 pg of carbon a year into the deep sea in the aftermath of voracious seasonal blooms. Up to 2-3x a year, rapid cycles of production and loss form living mosaics visable from Space. These transient works of art recieve unique pigmentation by 2000+ species of Phytoplanktonbased on sunlight and nutrient avialability and regulate the chemical currency of Ocean's carbon cycle. When supplies are in excess, the bloom and bust of these microscopic plants can amass a chemical deficits leading to hypoxia and Ocean acidification.

Ocean Acidification

The Atmosphere and Ocean actively compete for equal treatment. This childish dispute is especially problematic in the wake of rising atmospheric CO2 levels. As the Ocean surface races to dissolve CO2, reactions occur in the seawater creating corrosive conditions that deplete the resources necessary for biogenic calcification. For this reason, Ocean acidification threatens the seafood and nursery ecosystems that support the United States’ 35 billion dollar fishing and aquaculture industry.

Arctic Circulation

The ins-and-outs of this tiny land-enclosed sea are notoriously complex. Warm surface waters enter from the Pacific and Atlantic, cool, sink and exit through the Atlantic. The formation of these cold deep waters moderate the Global heat flux. As the Arctic warms, the influx of fresh meltwater creates profound feedback-loops on reigonal sea ice and global weather. Tracing the origins and pathways of these feedbacks involves understanding Arctic circulation, which can be done using reigonal relationships between oxygen-18 and salinity.

Next

Next Steps

As Arctic sea ice cover hit its fourth lowest on record, lucrative fossil reserves and shipping routes have enticed a meeting of World leaders. However, the retreating ice has largely unknown biogeochemical, ecological and economic reprecussions. The Arctic is warming twice as fast as the planet as a whole. Sea ice is predicted to disappear by the middle of the century. To discern what's to come, nuances in data analysis, remote sensing and numerical modeling must be applied to understand the interface between the sea, ice and air (see Hope and Schaefer 2015).
